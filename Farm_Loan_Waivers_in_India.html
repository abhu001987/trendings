Farm Loan Waivers in India

Understanding the impact of loan waivers on agriculture, banking, and fiscal policy

The Government of Maharashtra recently announced the ₹35,000 crore Punyashlok Ahilyadevi Holkar Farmers Loan Waiver Scheme. The scheme waives overdue crop loans up to ₹2 lakh (as of 30 September 2025) and provides incentives of up to ₹50,000 for regular loan repayers.

This marks Maharashtra’s third farm loan waiver in a decade, raising concerns about its impact on credit discipline, fiscal stability, and the long-term health of agricultural finance.

Key Concern:
The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) and several expert committees warn that frequent loan waivers can weaken the rural credit ecosystem and reduce banks’ willingness to lend.

What is a Farm Loan Waiver?

A farm loan waiver is a government policy in which the state repays farmers’ outstanding agricultural loans to banks and financial institutions. It is typically implemented during agrarian crises caused by crop failures, natural disasters, or price crashes.

Types of Farm Loan Waivers
  • Complete Waiver: Entire loan amount is written off for all farmers.
  • Partial Waiver: Loans are waived only up to a fixed limit (e.g., ₹1–2 lakh).
  • Targeted Waiver: Relief provided only to small or marginal farmers or specific regions.
  • Interest Waiver: Only the interest portion is waived, while the principal must still be repaid.

Historical Context in India

Major Central Loan Waivers
  • Agriculture and Rural Debt Relief Scheme (1990): Relief up to ₹10,000 per farmer, costing around ₹10,000 crore.
  • Agricultural Debt Waiver and Debt Relief Scheme (2008): Cost about ₹52,500 crore and targeted small and marginal farmers.

Since 2014–15, loan waivers have increasingly been announced by state governments including Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Uttar Pradesh, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Punjab, Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, and Tamil Nadu.

Over the past 35 years, governments in India have spent nearly ₹3 lakh crore on farm loan waiver schemes.

Implications of Rising Farm Loan Waivers

Positive Impacts

  • Immediate Relief: Provides financial support to farmers facing crop losses or price shocks.
  • Breaking the Debt Trap: Farmers regain eligibility for fresh credit for the next cropping season.
  • Boost to Rural Demand: Debt relief increases disposable income and rural consumption.
  • Psychological Relief: Reduces financial stress linked to farmer suicides caused by indebtedness.

Negative Impacts

  • Weakening Credit Discipline: Farmers may intentionally delay repayment expecting future waivers.
  • Rising NPAs: Agricultural NPAs reached about 8.44% in March 2019.
  • Fiscal Burden: Waivers consume between 0.1%–1.8% of GSDP of state budgets.
  • Poor Targeting: Many small farmers relying on informal lenders do not benefit.
  • Political Incentives: Many waivers are announced close to elections.
  • Crowding Out Investment: High government borrowing reduces private sector investment.

Alternatives to Farm Loan Waivers

Long-Term Solutions
  • Direct income support schemes like PM-Kisan.
  • Strengthening crop insurance through Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana.
  • Investment in irrigation, cold storage, and rural infrastructure.
  • Improved market access through platforms like e-NAM.
  • Affordable institutional credit through Kisan Credit Card.
  • Promotion of climate-resilient agriculture.
  • Diversification into dairy, fisheries, and agro-processing.

Conclusion

Farm loan waivers can provide short-term relief to distressed farmers but often weaken credit discipline and strain public finances. Sustainable solutions require structural reforms that increase farm income through better infrastructure, risk protection, and improved market access.

Long-term agricultural stability depends on strengthening the rural credit system rather than relying on repeated debt waivers.
